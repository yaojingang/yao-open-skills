# Editorial Production System

This guide controls the visual quality of DOCX, PDF, HTML, and chapter visuals. The priority order is:

1. `frontend-slides`: content density, responsive `clamp()` sizing, strong hierarchy, no generic AI aesthetic, no overflow-prone layouts
2. `kami`: Chinese/English document typography, warm paper restraint, ink-blue accent discipline, formal PDF/Word polish
3. `yao-ppt-skill`: guizang/e-ink-inspired editorial rhythm, image-first planning, and quality gates

Use these ideas as constraints, not decoration.

## Default Visual Direction

Default to a `frontend-slides`-first teaching report, not a decorative paper artifact:

- every visible element must serve real tutorial content or navigation
- use a clean centered report shell: sticky text navigation on the left, reading column on the right, and the two columns centered together as one layout
- use `clamp()` for major type and spacing
- use strong typographic hierarchy instead of decorative frames
- use one accent color, preferably ink blue `#1B365D`, under 5 percent of the page
- use warm off-white only as a quiet background; avoid visible paper textures unless the user asks for a document-artifact style
- use serif for titles/headings and readable sans for Chinese body text
- use hairline rules and light borders; avoid hard shadows, heavy cards, and ornamental surfaces
- keep corner radius `6-8px` for framed visuals and tables

Avoid:

- purple-blue gradient defaults
- glassmorphism
- thick shadows
- background grids or paper textures in formal output unless they encode content
- decorative blobs
- emoji icons
- repeated generic cards
- loose blog-like spacing in long documents
- visible helper labels such as "generated by", "visual pack", or instructions inside the final artifact
- absolute local filesystem paths in the visible source appendix

## Element Budget

Treat the final output as a formal deliverable. Remove anything that is not content, navigation, citation, or a teaching visual.

Allowed persistent elements:

- document title
- sticky anchor-text table of contents
- section headings
- body text
- tables, callouts, source IDs
- chapter visuals and captions
- source appendix, with local source files cited by readable labels only; keep absolute audit paths in internal research notes, not final deliverables

Not allowed in final output:

- duplicate title blocks
- decorative subtitles not supplied by the tutorial
- generic "Contents" badges when the anchor text menu is already clear
- visible usage instructions for the HTML report
- background patterns that do not carry information
- extra wrappers around visuals that do not improve reading
- repeated card surfaces around ordinary paragraphs

## Typography

### Chinese

- Title and major headings: `Noto Serif SC`, `Source Han Serif SC`, `Songti SC`, or another serious serif fallback.
- Body: `Noto Sans SC`, `Source Han Sans SC`, `PingFang SC`, `Microsoft YaHei`, or system CJK sans.
- Metadata and source IDs: `IBM Plex Mono`, `JetBrains Mono`, `SFMono-Regular`, or monospace fallback.

### English

- Title and major headings: a serif/editorial face such as `Newsreader`, `Charter`, `Georgia`.
- Body: a restrained sans or serif depending on the document tone.
- Avoid using Inter/Roboto/Arial as the main visual identity, though they are acceptable fallbacks.

## Tutorial Depth And Completeness

Default tutorial length should be substantial enough to teach. Do not cap a complete tutorial at a fixed word count unless the user explicitly asks for a short artifact.

Use learning sufficiency as the depth standard:

- The tutorial should be as long as needed for the learner to understand the problem, build the mental model, see examples, practice the method, and self-check results.
- The chapter count should follow the learning path, not a default quota. Start from `6-10` chapters for broad topics, then expand or merge based on prerequisite load and learner questions.
- Each chapter should feel complete on its own: scene challenge, explanation, example or case, visual, pitfall, practice/checkpoint, and a success state.
- Middle and later chapters must not become outline-like, compressed, or less careful than early chapters. If the back half becomes thin, revise chapter by chapter before export.
- Short versions are allowed only when the user asks for a sample, brief, one-pager, or fast draft.
- Deep manuals and course-style outputs may become long. Keep structure, navigation, and pacing strong rather than cutting necessary explanation.
- Final practice section: at least one applied exercise and one self-check rubric.

## HTML Report Contract

The final HTML report must feel like a designed reading artifact:

- Include a table of contents generated from headings.
- Style `nav#TOC` as a plain anchor-text menu, not as a decorative card.
- On desktop, place `nav#TOC` and the article body inside one centered `report-shell` grid. The menu and reading column should feel like one balanced container, not two independently positioned elements.
- Make the menu sticky on the left side while content scrolls.
- Keep the title area compact: H1, date line, one divider, then opening text. A long table of contents must stay outside the article body so it cannot create a large blank area below the title.
- On mobile, make the menu a compact sticky top block.
- Highlight hierarchy with chapter numbers, short headings, and visible source/caption rhythm.
- Use textbook-style numbering: `第1章 章节标题` for H2 and `1.1 小节标题` for H3. The same visible numbering must carry through HTML, PDF, and DOCX.
- Use chapter-aware visual captions when helpful, such as `图 1.1：...` for the first figure in chapter 1.
- Keep the reading column between `700px` and `860px`.
- Keep the combined desktop layout width restrained: table of contents around `196px-252px`, a responsive gap, and the reading column around `820px`, centered within the viewport.
- Use responsive type with `clamp()`.
- Use `scroll-margin-top` on headings so anchor jumps do not hide content behind sticky chrome.
- For print/PDF, avoid sticky UI chrome; PDF should read like a clean document.
- Do not create a duplicate title block. The markdown `H1` is the visible title; export metadata should only set the browser/PDF page title. A single date line below H1 is allowed.

## DOCX And PDF Contract

DOCX and PDF should be generated from the same markdown source, but they need document-specific discipline:

- Use a Word reference document when available.
- Keep Word and PDF clean: no running headers, no running footers, no file paths, no browser-generated page counters.
- Heading styles should be hierarchical and compact.
- Body line-height should feel editorial, not web-loose.
- Images should be high resolution and centered, with captions directly below.
- Tables should use light borders and restrained header shading.
- Use Kami-style warm parchment `#f5f4ed`, ivory `#faf9f5`, warm borders, and one ink-blue accent. Avoid pure white table slabs.
- HTML image figures must reset browser-default `figure` margins.
- Do not add an extra border around chapter screenshots when the screenshot already contains an artboard frame.
- If markdown images create automatic pandoc figcaptions and the tutorial also has a `图 N` caption, hide the automatic figcaption and keep the authored caption.
- Long HTML tables must be wrapped in a horizontal-scroll container with stable borders, row rhythm, and readable cell wrapping.
- Avoid manual page-break hacks unless a layout issue is visible.
- PDF should be rendered from the polished HTML after visuals are embedded.

## Visual Artifact Contract

Every chapter visual should be a teaching object:

- One visual explains one idea.
- Use the same frontend-slides-first visual system as the document.
- Treat chapter visuals as a real canvas system: artboard, nodes, edges, groups, axes, marks, and annotations.
- Prefer diagrams, models, matrices, flows, layered stacks, and comparison charts over decorative illustrations.
- Titles should be short enough to fit in one or two lines.
- Captions should tell the reader what to notice.
- Use high-resolution screenshots: default capture should be at least `2x` scale.
- Keep diagram labels concise; if the label needs a paragraph, the diagram is too dense.
- Avoid stock-like atmosphere. The visual must reveal a real relationship, process, model, or tradeoff.
- Avoid large empty poster-like whitespace unless the whitespace clarifies hierarchy.
- Avoid using two accent colors to imply categories unless the data genuinely has two categories.
- Prefer one focal accent and neutral secondary nodes.
- Prefer deterministic layouts inspired by open-source diagram tools over arbitrary placement.
- Do not use the same box-and-arrow template for every chapter; vary the visual grammar according to the teaching job.

## Rhythm Rules

Borrow presentation rhythm without turning the tutorial into slides:

- Open with a strong hook, then slow down into explanation.
- Alternate dense teaching chapters with lighter examples, cases, or diagrams.
- Avoid three consecutive sections with the same structure.
- Use callouts sparingly: checkpoint, common pitfall, what to notice.
- Keep source appendix audit-friendly but visually quieter than teaching content.

## Production Quality Gate

Before delivery, check:

- body depth matches the requested learning outcome and is not artificially compressed by a word-count ceiling
- each numbered chapter has an internal quality review in `research/chapter-quality-review.md`
- middle and later chapters are not noticeably thinner or less actionable than early chapters
- every chapter has a visual and caption
- screenshots are crisp enough for PDF/Word
- HTML image frames do not show double borders or duplicate captions
- HTML tables do not overflow, collapse awkwardly, or squeeze long Chinese text into unreadable columns
- Word and PDF have no visible headers or footers
- HTML has a sticky anchor menu
- HTML desktop layout centers the table of contents and reading column together as one `report-shell`, instead of pinning the menu to the viewport edge
- HTML has no duplicate visible title
- PDF opens and images are visible
- DOCX opens and contains embedded images
- no absolute filesystem paths appear in final HTML, PDF, or DOCX
- typography, color, captions, and tables follow the same design system
- no visible element is decorative-only
